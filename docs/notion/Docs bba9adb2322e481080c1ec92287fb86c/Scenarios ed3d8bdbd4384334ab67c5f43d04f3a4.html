David

It's 3 weeks before the quarantine has started, people are worried, but most businesses haven't enacted full-time work from home. Your team has been working on a software communications app, anticipating that it will be able to take a giant market share if it can be released before Microsoft teams and slack update their applications, as your app has features they lack. Your team is ready to release. Let's say the backend is rooted heavily in a framework, call it pumpanickel (made up name). You come across an article early one morning written by a freelance software engineer, who claims they have found a severe security flaw in pumpanickel, allowing database tables to be retrieved without any sort of security checks. In your team's app, this could mean an outsider could retrieve entire tables of messages within teams, and even tables that include user emails. The current recommended fix is to switch over from pumpanickel to weet, another made up (but considered secure) backend framework. Switching your team's codebase to weet would require delaying release by at least a month, giving slack or microsoft teams valuable time to release an update that could provide the sought-after features people want, which your app has. What is the correct course of action?

Daniel

Your small independent game developer team is using Scrum, and you are approaching the end of the current sprint based on the plan your team has laid out for the past 2 weeks. You are working on a multiplayer first person shooter game called "Modern Minecraft Eternal", and the game is fairly close to completion and ready for release. This sprint, you were responsible for writing the netcode for coordinating player actions and third person viewmodels (the model other players will see when they look at you.) Unfortunately for you, you already failed to deliever code you promised for the last sprint, and this sprint you decided to sleep 22 hours a day for the past 14 days and were unable to come up with any satisfactory code, not even a skeleton. Luckily, or unluckily, during your research you happened to come across leaked source code for a similar multiplayer first person shooter game called "ROBLOX: White Ops" that contains a fleshed out and robust system for handling third person viewmodels based on player actions. The source code for "ROBLOX: White Ops" is already a few years old, but it appears the game has already been released and is still being developed by its creators.

Given that you already failed to deliver code in the last sprint, it is likely that if you do not decide to use the code this sprint, you will suffer some punishment (fiscal or possibly dismissal from the company), and forced to look for another job in the current COVID-19 affected climate. "Modern Minecraft Eternal" is already only 2-3 more sprints away from completion, and you will be able to enjoy a potentially large playerbase thanks to everyone being stuck at home. You do not want to lose your stake in this project if at all possible.

If you decide to use the code for your own development, it is likely that you will be able to finish the necessary netcode by the end of the sprint, but the engine your team will base their development upon will now contain code that is technically owned by another company. If the developers of "ROBLOX: White Ops" happen to notice that you used their code (in the same way you happened to come across their leaked source code), it is probable that you may suffer unforeseen consequences (both legal and fiscal.) For reference, the current legal case Google v. Oracle America is a similar and real scenario that Oracle is seeking $8.8 billion for in damages. (see https://fossbytes.com/9-lines-of-code-that-google-stole-from-oracle-java-android/)

What will you do?

Mitchell

Suppose you are the team leader of a group of 10 people. This includes 1 team lead, 2 research, 1 build, 2 testers, and 4 coders. Each person is assigned one role. You are about 70% through the project, and are slightly behind schedule, but would most likely still be able to complete the project by the deadline.

However, the main coder for the front-end has a medical emergency and will be unable to continue for the foreseeable future. He was close to finishing, but is still not done. The project must ship in 2 weeks with a front-end, but no one else is familiar with it or has much experience. It would take at least one person to absorb his role, and more likely more than one to comfortably accomplish his tasks before the deadline. How do you, as the team leader, resolve this scenario?

Solutions:

Have a person from research and have a person on tester both work on the task he was supposed to finish. This may compromise the quality of the project, as a lack of person in testing may cause more bugs to remain undetected. However, since this is the first ship, these bugs can be fixed later on.

Take up that person’s task yourself

This would allow people to remain on the other teams, and thereby ensuring the quality of the overall project to remain as intact as possible. The tradeoff may be a lack of leadership and communication, without a team leader focusing on directing the group as a whole.

Delay shipping of the project and have available team members/coders work on his task as needed.

The customer may be angry and disappointed. If the customer is in a rush, delaying the project may deem it even unusable for him/her. It may also cost him/her lots of money. He/She may not want to do business with you anymore in the future. However, this would ensure the quality of the project when it is shipped.

Patrick

Pablo is a software engineer that is a recent hire for SoftWare Inc that has done some great work for the company in their front-end development. He was called into his team manager’s office one day and was told he would be up for a promotion with a higher pay and moved to a different department. If he were to be promoted, then he would have the choice of choosing between two departments in the company.

The first department, Alpha, is supervised by Kara, a new, college-aged manager fresh out of school who is known to supervise her workers very well. She understands her workers well and balances being productive and mental health. The department is known for its productivity and team morale is seemingly at an all time high. However, due to external factors, people who work in this department are not considered as much to be promoted to higher paying positions. In addition, this department gets all the R&D for the company which is considered to be difficult work.

The second department, Beta, is supervised by Devin, a senior, tenured manager who has been at the company since its creation. Devin is known to be a manager who is very short-sighted and only wants things done his way. Very micro-managerial and gets angry at employees who make any mistake. People who are in this department are greatly considered for higher paying positions in the company as compared to the Alpha department. In addition, this department gets the IT service projects which are considered to be easy.

What should Pablo do in this situation?

Solution 1: Go to Alpha Department

  • Pablo gets the benefit of being in a great department with high team morale and dynamics
  • He also gets a team manager that is understanding and is able to accelerate the productivity of Pablo
  • Downside is that there is no potential chance of promoting again if placed in this department unless he becomes a star standout or picked for favorites
  • Also, he would get more difficult work to deal with but could be potentially in the creation of new works/inventions/software
  • He would also have a manager that would know how to deal with the difficulties of the projects and have the ability to thrive.
  • Immense growth as an engineer since he is able to flex his engineering mind and think

Solution 2: Go to Beta Department

  • Able to promote much easier to a even higher position
  • Easier work to deal with in the company
  • Little growth at all as a engineer in terms of learning new things
  • Great growth in learning how to deal with stubborn managers
  • Terrible team manager that is oppressive and mean, which can lead to no productivity at all due to mismanagement
  • Possibility of being fired quicker if on the bad side of the team manager

Solution 3: Do not go to any department and stay at current position

  • Does not have to make the choice of picking a department
  • Sticks with a comfortable position that he is already in
  • There is safety and reliance on things he already knew
  • No potential to grow in the company, and potential to grow as an engineer as well
  • No risk taking
